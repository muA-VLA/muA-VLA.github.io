µA-VLA:
A Multi-Agent Online Self-Healing Framework for Long-Horizon Robotic Manipulation

Ziheng Liu1, You Wang2, Jun Fu1
1Zhejiang Gongshang University   2Luoteng Technology (Hangzhou) Co., Ltd. & Zhejiang University
Paper Code Video

Abstract

Recent advances in vision-language-action (VLA) models have improved robotic manipulation, yet they remain brittle in long-horizon, multi-step execution where errors accumulate and lead to deadlocks or premature termination. We introduce µA-VLA, a multi-agent online self-healing framework that couples failure reasoning from failure visual observations with dynamic, code-driven task intervention in a reasoning–coding–intervention loop. Built on top of π0.5 as the execution backbone and instantiating all agents with Claude Opus 4.6, µA-VLA diagnoses execution anomalies, synthesizes runnable recovery code, and performs targeted online interventions to resume and sustain task progress. Extensive evaluations show that µA-VLA achieves new state-of-the-art performance on the CALVIN ABC→D benchmark, reaching an Avg. Len. of 4.76 with 90.1% 5-step success, alongside robust real-world long-horizon deployments.

Method

µA-VLA augments a fixed VLA controller with an online self-healing loop. Given the task instruction, subtask context, and failure visual observations, the framework produces a structured failure analysis record that captures the key symptom, relevant constraints, and plausible physical cause. It then synthesizes executable Python code to instantiate a task-specific intervention agent, which generates intervention instructions and invokes the underlying VLA tools to adjust the environment or execution order. The execution outcome is written back to the records, enabling continual refinement of recovery strategies across long-horizon rollouts.

Results

Main benchmark results. On CALVIN ABC→D, µA-VLA sets a new state of the art across long-horizon metrics, achieving an Avg. Len. of 4.76 and 90.1% 5-step success. Compared with strong recent baselines, the gains are most pronounced at longer horizons, indicating that µA-VLA effectively mitigates error accumulation during chained execution.

Results

Per-subtask improvements. We further analyze performance at the subtask level. µA-VLA consistently increases success rates on representative subtasks, especially those that frequently fail due to missing preconditions, occlusions, or unstable interactions. These improvements reflect the benefit of converting failure understanding into targeted online interventions.

Results

Agent configuration study. We evaluate different agent configurations by varying the underlying LLM/VLM choices. While stronger core models yield higher performance, µA-VLA remains consistently strong across configurations. Notably, even the weakest configuration achieves an Avg. Len. exceeding the prior SoTA (FLOWER: 4.53), demonstrating the robustness of the proposed closed-loop collaboration.

Results

Real-world long-horizon deployment. We validate µA-VLA on real-robot long-horizon tasks with varied sequence lengths and orderings. µA-VLA reliably recovers from execution anomalies and continues task progress, showing strong deployability beyond simulation under practical sensory noise and physical uncertainties.

Results

Sequence-level analysis. We test long-horizon subtask sequences of length 3–5. In challenging sequences where the backbone policy can deadlock due to unmet preconditions, µA-VLA diagnoses the bottleneck and inserts corrective actions online to complete the full sequence. In contrast, for well-conditioned sequences where preconditions are naturally satisfied by the ordering, the backbone succeeds without intervention, highlighting when online recovery is most beneficial.
